# Technical vs adaptive classification

Diagnose which kind of work a recurring gap is (Heifetz). The split shapes *what
the quiz reinforces*, not whether to coach.

- **Technical challenge:** a known answer exists and current expertise can apply
  it. If the person already understands it, there is nothing to coach. If they
  keep getting it wrong, the quiz reinforces the correct understanding.
- **Adaptive challenge:** progress needs the person to change a value, belief,
  habit, loyalty, or way of working. More information alone cannot solve it,
  because the gap is between the values the person holds and the reality they
  face. The quiz builds the person's own judgement.

## The diagnostic question

One test, drawn straight from Heifetz, decides the split:

> **Does making real progress on this gap require the person to change their
> values, beliefs, habits, priorities, or ways of working — or can it be closed
> by applying knowledge they could simply be given?**

If applying known expertise closes it, the gap is **technical**. If it can only
close when the person themselves changes, it is **adaptive**. Heifetz defines
adaptive work as *"the learning required to address conflicts in the values
people hold, or to diminish the gap between the values people stand for and the
reality they face"* — learning the person must do, not information a coach can
hand over.

## It is a spectrum, not a binary

Heifetz frames the split through a doctor–patient analogy with **three** types,
because the operative variable is *who must do the work* to close the gap:

| Type | Problem definition | Solution & implementation | Who holds the work |
|------|--------------------|---------------------------|--------------------|
| **I — technical** | Clear | Clear, applied by an expert | The authority/expert ("the physician fixes it") |
| **II — mixed** | Clear | Requires the person's own learning to implement | Shared: the expert advises, but only the person can carry it out (e.g. heart disease — the diagnosis is clear, but recovery needs the patient to change diet, exercise, habits) |
| **III — adaptive** | Requires learning | Requires learning | The person, primarily; the coach mobilises the learning rather than supplying the answer |

Most real recurring gaps are **Type II or III**, not the clean Type I a quick
glance suggests. Naming the middle (Type II) matters: a gap can have a known
technical component *and* an adaptive core, and aiming the quiz at the technical
half while the adaptive half is what actually stalls progress is the same
mislabel in disguise.

## Locus of work — why this determines the intervention

The classification is not academic; it dictates whether a quiz is even the right
move. For a **technical** gap, the locus of responsibility sits with expertise —
the right response may simply be to supply the correct answer. For an
**adaptive** gap, the work belongs to the person with the problem. Heifetz calls
the move **"giving the work back to the people, at a rate they can stand"** —
which is exactly what a prosthesis-building quiz does: it returns the call to the
person instead of making it for them. A quiz aimed at a purely technical gap
just withholds an answer that should be given; a quiz aimed at an adaptive gap
builds durable judgement.

## Diagnostic signs a gap is adaptive

Tells that a recurring gap is adaptive rather than technical:

- It **persists despite repeated technical fixes** — new tools, more
  information, and process tweaks have not closed it.
- Closing it would require the person to **give something up** — a habit, a
  comfort, an identity, a loyalty — not just learn a fact.
- It carries **emotional weight or resistance** disproportionate to a simple
  knowledge gap.
- The person reaches for **work avoidance** (below) instead of the call itself.

## Work avoidance — how the mislabel shows up

Adaptive work is uncomfortable, so people unconsciously divert from it. Heifetz
names these **work-avoidance** patterns, and the most relevant one here is
**recasting an adaptive challenge as a technical one** — because it lets the
person look busy without doing the harder change. Watch for:

- **Pretending the problem is technical** — "we just need a better tool / more
  data / a new process" when the real gap is a call only the person can own.
- **Looking to authority for the easy answer** — handing the decision to a
  manager, an expert, or the agent.
- **Denial, scapegoating, or externalising** — locating the problem anywhere but
  in the change the person must make.

These tells are how you catch the mislabel in the accumulated signal (e.g.
`authority-dependence`, `avoidance` categories in the store).

## Name the split

Mislabeling an adaptive challenge as a technical one is, in Heifetz's words, *the
single most common source of leadership failure* — and a quiz aimed at the wrong
kind of gap will not stick. State the result, and which of the three types it
is, in the **Classification** heading of the output.
