# Technical vs adaptive classification

Diagnose which kind of work a recurring gap is. The split shapes *what the quiz
reinforces*, not whether to coach. The frame is Ronald Heifetz's: misdiagnosing
the kind of work is, in his words, the most common source of leadership failure
(Heifetz & Linsky, *Leadership on the Line*, 2002), so this classification is the
load-bearing step — get it wrong and a well-built quiz still aims at the wrong
target.

## What adaptive work actually is

> Adaptive work consists of the learning required to address conflicts in the
> values people hold, or to diminish the gap between the values people stand for
> and the reality they face.
> — Heifetz, *Leadership Without Easy Answers*, 1994

The operational test: **is the obstacle a missing fact, or a gap between what the
person values and what they actually do?** A fact can be supplied. A values–reality
gap can only be closed by the person changing a priority, habit, belief, or
behaviour — no amount of information substitutes for that learning.

## Three kinds of work, not two

Heifetz, Grashow & Linsky (*The Practice of Adaptive Leadership*, 2009) classify
work along three axes — who can define the problem, who can supply the solution,
and where the work has to be done. Most real challenges are **mixed**; forcing a
binary is itself a common mislabel.

| Kind of challenge       | Problem definition | Solution & implementation | Primary locus of the work |
| ----------------------- | ------------------ | ------------------------- | ------------------------- |
| Technical               | Clear              | Clear                     | Authority / expert        |
| Technical *and* adaptive | Clear              | Requires learning         | Authority *and* stakeholders |
| Adaptive                | Requires learning  | Requires learning         | Stakeholders (those with the problem) |

- **Technical:** a known answer exists and an expert can apply it. If the person
  already understands it, there is nothing to coach. If they keep getting it
  wrong, the quiz reinforces the correct, knowable understanding.
- **Technical and adaptive:** the fix is partly known but lands only if the person
  also changes how they work. Coach the adaptive half — the part that requires
  their own learning — and let the technical half be answered.
- **Adaptive:** progress needs the person to change a value, habit, belief, or
  behaviour. More information alone cannot solve it; the quiz builds the person's
  own judgement so they make the call themselves next time (the prosthesis
  effect).

## Diagnostic clues: how to tell it is adaptive

Heifetz's tells for adaptive (vs. technical) work — use these on the accumulated
signal, not a single instance:

1. **The technical fix keeps failing.** The same expert/tool answer is applied
   repeatedly and the problem returns — a sign the real work is being avoided.
   (Maps to the `mislabeled-technical` category.)
2. **A values–reality gap, not a knowledge gap.** The person knows what to do and
   still does not do it; the obstacle is priorities or loyalties, not facts.
   (`values-conflict`.)
3. **The solution requires loss.** Progress means giving something up — a comfort,
   an identity, a way of working — so resistance is emotional, not informational.
   (`loss-aversion`.)
4. **The work belongs to the person, not the authority.** It cannot be delegated
   to the agent/expert without recreating dependence; only the person who holds
   the problem can do the learning. (`authority-dependence`.)
5. **Avoidance behaviour is present** (see below). (`avoidance`, `no-experiment`.)

If none of these hold, treat it as technical and simply reinforce the right
answer.

## Work avoidance — the loudest adaptive signal

Because adaptive work is uncomfortable, people reliably divert it rather than do
it (Heifetz, *Leadership Without Easy Answers*, 1994). Recurring avoidance is
strong evidence the gap is adaptive, not technical. Common forms:

- **Displacement onto a technical fix** — "we just need a better tool/process"
  for what is really a judgement call (the classic mislabel).
- **Handing the decision to an authority** — asking the agent/expert to "just
  decide" what is the person's own call to own.
- **Denial, scapegoating, or stalling** — keeping the problem unstated so the
  uncomfortable learning never has to happen.

Name the avoidance gently and without shame: the goal is to give the work back to
the person, not to indict them.

## Stating the result

Name the split in the **Classification** heading of the output. Be explicit about
mixed cases — "the tooling is technical and solvable; the recurring stall is the
adaptive call being handed off" — because separating the two is what lets the quiz
aim at the part that actually requires the person's learning.
